## Grammar Review

### 1) `walk around` and `work around` are different

- If you mean 四处走走: `Do you want to walk around?`
- If you mean 绕开问题: `We need to work around the issue.`
- Note: `walk` is movement; `work around` is problem-solving.

### 2) `work out` fits exercise

- Natural: `Walking is my favorite way to work out.`
- Also natural for casual walking: `Walking around is my favorite thing to do.`
- Note: `work out` = 锻炼, while `walk around` = 四处走走.

### 3) Add `a` before a singular countable noun

- Less natural: `Do you want to get a job with good salary?`
- Correct: `Do you want to get a job with a good salary?`
- Note: `salary` is a singular countable noun here, so it needs an article.

### 4) `You did nothing.` is grammatical but strong in tone

- Correct: `You did nothing.`
- Meaning: 你什么都没做。
- Note: This sounds direct and blaming. Softer options include `You didn't do anything.` or `Nothing was done.`

### 5) This apology sentence is natural and polite

- Correct: `I'm sorry I didn't meet your expectations.`
- Meaning: 很抱歉，我没有达到你的期望 / 没有让你满意。
- Also useful: `I'm sorry I couldn't meet your expectations.`

### 6) Simple past is often better for a finished purchase

- Grammatical: `I've purchased this metro card.`
- More natural in conversation: `I purchased this metro card.` / `I bought this metro card.`
- Note: Use simple past more often when the buying action is a finished past event.

### 7) Add `an` before `iPhone` in a title

- Better title: `How to take a compliant passport photo with an iPhone at home`
- Note: `compliant` means 符合规范的；here it means the photo meets official requirements.

## Pronunciation Tips

- `walk` /wɔːk/ vs `work` /wɜːrk/: the vowel is the key difference. `walk` is rounder and more open; `work` is tighter and more central.
- `archive` /ˈɑːrkaɪv/: stress the first syllable, not the second.
- `tramway` /ˈtræmweɪ/: say `tram` clearly, then connect to `way`.
- `garage` /ɡəˈrɑːʒ/: in American English, the stress is usually on the second syllable.
- `vessel` /ˈvesəl/: the first syllable sounds like `ves`, not `vee`.
