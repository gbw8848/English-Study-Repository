## Grammar Review

### 1) Use `It's time for + noun`, but `It's time to + verb`

- Less natural: `It's time dinner now.`
- Incorrect: `It's time to dinner.`
- Better: `It's time for dinner now.`
- Also correct: `It's time to eat dinner now.`
- Note: `dinner` is a noun, so it goes after `for`. After `to`, use a verb.

### 2) Use `have been wanting to`, not `have been wanna to`

- Incorrect: `I've been wanna to try face spray.`
- Better: `I've been wanting to try face spray.`
- Also natural: `I've wanted to try face spray for a while.`
- Note: after `have been`, use the `-ing` form here: `wanting`.

### 3) `affect` is usually the verb; `effect` is usually the noun

- Correct: `The weather really affects my mood.`
- Less natural here: `The weather really effects my mood.`
- Noun example: `The weather has a big effect on my mood.`
- Note: for your sentence, `affects` is the right choice.

### 4) Match singular and plural nouns carefully

- Incorrect: `I saw these box.`
- Better: `I saw these boxes.`
- Also correct: `I saw this box.`
- Note: `these` goes with plural nouns, and `this` goes with singular nouns.

### 5) `make up` and `makeup` are not the same

- `make up` = a verb phrase, often meaning `reconcile`, `invent`, or `complete later`
- `makeup` = a noun meaning `cosmetics`
- If you mean 化妆, say: `It's time to put on makeup.` or `It's time to do my makeup.`
- `It's time to make up.` usually means `该和好了`, not `该化妆了`.

### 6) `tend to` and `gravitate toward` can work together naturally

- Natural: `I tend to gravitate toward more natural colors.`
- Meaning: I usually and naturally prefer more natural colors.
- Note: `tend to` adds the habit idea, while `gravitate toward` adds the feeling of being drawn to something.

## Pronunciation Tips

- `tip` /tɪp/ and `tape` /teɪp/ are very different: short `i` vs long `a`.
- `Trustpilot` is one word. Say it as `TRUST-pilot`, not `trust pilot` as two separate words.
- `well` /wel/, `wheel` /wiːl/, and `view` /vjuː/ need three clearly different vowel sounds.
- `gold` /ɡoʊld/ is different from `god` /ɡɑːd/ and `good` /ɡʊd/.
- `gloomy` /ˈɡluːmi/ is the weather word here; `glowy` and `glorious` mean something different.
